There is a thin line between discipline and oppression. And in many situations, that line seems to have been crossed.

Employees are not machines. Nor are they property to be controlled at will. Yet in practice, policies often treat them as if they have no life beyond their work.

Schedules are changed unilaterally. Activities are imposed without room for objection. Attendance is enforced—even for matters that are not always directly relevant to core responsibilities.

All of this operates under a dangerous assumption: that employees must always be available, always compliant, and need not be considered as individuals.

The question is simple—yet rarely answered honestly:

Is this still management, or has it become excessive control?

Even more ironic, these practices are often wrapped in noble-sounding terms: togetherness, loyalty, commitment. But forced togetherness is not togetherness. Pressured loyalty is not loyalty. It is compliance born out of pressure.

And pressure, in the long run, never produces quality. It only breeds exhaustion, apathy, and a loss of belonging.

What is most concerning is not the workload itself, but the erosion of basic humanity.

Not all employees live under the same conditions. Some must divide their time with family. Some carry the responsibilities of parenthood. Others are quietly struggling to maintain their physical and mental well-being.

Yet the system looks away.

Everyone is treated the same. Everyone is assumed capable. Everyone is expected to comply—without being understood.

This is where an organization begins to lose its direction.

Because a healthy organization is not measured solely by how obedient its employees are, but by how fairly it treats them as human beings.

If policies continue to operate without empathy, what remains is merely a structure without a soul.

And such a structure may still stand—but it will slowly lose its meaning.

This is not an act of defiance. It is a warning.

That when people are no longer treated as human, what breaks is not only the individual—but the system itself.
